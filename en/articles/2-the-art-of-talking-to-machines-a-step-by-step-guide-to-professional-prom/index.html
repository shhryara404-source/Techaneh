The Art of Talking to Machines: A Step‑by‑Step Guide to Professional Prompt Engineering

You've probably chatted with a bot and received shallow answers. The root cause is improper prompt writing. In this article, learn the art of prompt engineering through a six‑step formula and advanced techniques...

Techaneh Editorial

You've likely experienced chatting with a bot like ChatGPT or Gemini, only to receive shallow, generic, or completely wrong answers. You might have wondered, 'Can't this AI understand what I mean?' The root of the problem is a big misconception: that AI understands words like a human. As we explained in the first article, language models convert words into numerical tokens and respond based on mathematical probabilities.

This is where Prompt Engineering comes in – the art of crafting effective instructions for AI models. In simple terms, prompt engineering means asking the right questions and giving the right commands to get the best possible results. Today, prompt engineering is no longer an optional skill; it's essential for everyone.

A prompt is the text or input a user gives to a large language model so that the model can generate a response. The goal is to provide enough information for the desired output.

The quality of the generated responses heavily depends on the quality of the input. When you apply prompt‑engineering principles, it's like programming in natural language – steering the model's mathematical probabilities with your words.

Based on Google's prompt engineering courses and other reliable sources, a professional prompt consists of six key components, in order of importance:

  1. Task – top priority and mandatory: always start with a precise action verb.
  2. Context – tell the whole story: background, definition of success, and the environment.
  3. Exemplars / Few‑shot – provide examples: a few input‑output pairs drastically improve quality.
  4. Persona – give the model an identity: specify the character or expertise to adopt.
  5. Format – define the output structure: table, JSON, paragraphs, or bullet list.
  6. Tone – set the mood: formal, casual, humorous, empathetic, or educational.

Golden tip: steering the model with details always yields better results than simple commanding.

Beyond the six‑step formula, there are advanced techniques:

  • Chain of Thought (CoT): ask the model to explain its reasoning step‑by‑step.
  • Zero‑Shot / One‑Shot: performing a task with no examples or just one example.
  • RAG (Retrieval‑Augmented Generation): combining external information retrieval with the prompt.

Common mistakes include overly short prompts, unclear instructions, and expecting miracles.

Example of a weak prompt: 'Write an Instagram post for my new product.' A professional prompt: 'As a content marketing specialist with 5 years of experience in the fashion industry, write an Instagram post to introduce a new leather handbag. Target audience: women aged 25–40. The tone should be warm, friendly, and inspiring while conveying a luxury brand feel. Start with a catchy headline, then a short story about the handbag's craftsmanship, and end with a call‑to‑action. Use three paragraphs of 2‑3 lines each.'

Prompt engineering is a skill – the more you practise, the better you become.
