From Dream to Reality: A Journey into the Digital Brain

Until a few years ago, artificial intelligence was confined to science fiction. Today, it permeates our daily lives.

Techaneh Editorial

Until a few years ago, the phrase 'intelligent machines' brought to mind sci‑fi movies and humanoid robots. Today, artificial intelligence is no longer a distant dream – it's a daily companion. From the moment your phone's voice assistant wakes you up to the personalized video recommendations on social media at night, AI leaves its footprint everywhere. But what exactly is this astonishing technology and how does it work? Let's embark on a journey into this digital brain.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is, in the simplest definition, the ability of a machine or computer system to think, learn, and make decisions. However, this simple definition conceals the true essence of the phenomenon. AI is actually a collection of mathematical and computational techniques and algorithms designed to mimic human intelligence and learning. In other words, the intelligence that a machine exhibits in various situations is what we call artificial intelligence.

Interestingly, AI as an academic discipline was first established in 1956 at the Dartmouth Summer Conference by John McCarthy. McCarthy, a computer and cognitive scientist, coined the term 'artificial intelligence'. Since then, the field has seen many ups and downs – sometimes waves of optimism, sometimes funding shortages and despair.

The ultimate goal of AI is to create software and hardware tools that can reason, make decisions, learn, and solve problems – sometimes even more accurately than humans. Until recently, many such tasks were solely human domains; today, we are witnessing advances once only imaginable in science fiction.

One common confusion in this field is the difference between artificial intelligence, machine learning, and deep learning. Imagine them as a three‑layer pyramid:

  • Artificial Intelligence (top, broadest layer): encompasses all efforts to build intelligent machines.
  • Machine Learning (middle layer): a subset of AI that gives machines the ability to learn from data without being explicitly programmed.
  • Deep Learning (bottom, most specialized layer): a subset of machine learning that uses multi‑layered neural networks to process data, inspired by the structure of the human brain.

AI systems, especially large language models like ChatGPT, don't understand words directly. They convert words into numerical codes called tokens and predict the next word based on mathematical probabilities. So when you chat with a bot, you are essentially conversing with an advanced probability calculator!

The process works by training models on vast amounts of data – text, images, audio, etc. During training, they identify hidden patterns, relationships, and structures. After training, when you give them a prompt, they use those learned patterns to generate the statistically most probable response.

AI is not limited to chatbots; it shines in many branches:

  • Natural Language Processing (NLP): understanding, interpreting, and generating human language – the power behind ChatGPT and Gemini.
  • Computer Vision: seeing and interpreting images and videos; from facial recognition in smartphones to self‑driving cars.
  • Robotics: combining AI with hardware to build robots that act in the physical world.
  • Recommendation Systems: algorithms that suggest relevant content on YouTube, Netflix, or Amazon based on your tastes and behaviour.

AI is not just a technology; it is a revolution that is reshaping how we work, live, and interact with the world. Understanding its fundamentals is the first and most crucial step toward using it professionally.
